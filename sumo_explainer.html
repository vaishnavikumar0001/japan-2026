01 / 10 — The Basics

What Is Sumo?

Japan's national sport — over 1,500 years old. Part athletic combat, part Shinto ritual. Every movement in the ring carries meaning rooted in ancient tradition.

⭕
The Ring (Dohyo)
A raised clay platform, ~4.5m across. The twisted straw rope marks the boundary. Step outside or touch down — you lose.
🏆
How to Win
Push or throw your opponent outside the ring, or make any body part (except feet soles) touch the ground. Simple rule, infinite strategy.
⚡
The Speed
Most top-level bouts last under 10 seconds. The pre-bout ritual lasts up to 4 minutes. The build-up IS the show.
02 / 10 — The Hierarchy

The Rank Pyramid

Sumo has 6 divisions. You're watching the top one — Makuuchi. Within it, rank determines who fights last (and who gets the most respect).

YOKOZUNA   横綱 Grand Champion — highest honour in all of sport
OZEKI   大関 Champion-level — the title hunters
SEKIWAKE   関脇 Third rank — the dangerous dark horses
KOMUSUBI   小結 Fourth rank — elite rising stars
MAEGASHIRA   前頭 Top division — still elite, ranked 1–17

Yokozuna can never be demoted — but they're expected to retire when their performance slips. The rope they wear in ceremony weighs 15kg.

05 / 10 — The Ritual

The Pre-Bout Ceremony

The ritual before each bout can last up to 4 minutes for Yokozuna. Don't mistake it for stalling — every single action is Shinto tradition dating back centuries.

Step 1
🧂 Salt Toss (Shio)
Wrestlers grab handfuls of salt and scatter it across the dohyo. Salt purifies the ring — a Shinto ritual to ward off evil and protect wrestlers from injury. Up to 40kg of salt is used per tournament day.
Step 2
🦵 Leg Stomping (Shiko)
High leg raises and powerful stomps into the clay. This drives evil spirits into the ground. It's also a display of strength and a physical warm-up. Yokozuna's shiko in their ring entry ceremony is dramatic and slow.
Step 3
🙌 Clap & Spread (Chirichouzu)
Wrestlers clap hands, spread arms wide showing open palms — a declaration that they carry no weapons. Pure sporting combat. Trust.
Step 4
👀 The Stare-Down (Niramiai)
Both wrestlers crouch at the white lines, making eye contact. They can back away and reset multiple times. Psychological warfare. When both touch fists to clay simultaneously — the match begins.
Launch
💥 Tachi-ai — The Initial Charge
Both wrestlers explode simultaneously from the crouch. This collision sets the tone for everything. A perfect tachi-ai can win the match before it feels like it's started. Watch their feet and hands in the first half-second.
06 / 10 — The Techniques

Key Moves to Spot

There are 82 official winning techniques (kimarite). Here are the most common ones you'll see — each announced by the gyōji referee after the bout.

Yorikiri
MOST COMMON
Frontal force-out. Grab the belt and walk your opponent backwards over the straw. Pure power and grip.
Hatakikomi
SLAP DOWN
Slap your charging opponent down to the clay as they rush forward. Looks easy — causes cushion throws if used on a Yokozuna.
Uwatenage
OVERARM THROW
Grab the belt with an outside grip and hurl your opponent in an arc. When it works it's spectacular. Hōshōryū's signature.
Oshidashi
FRONTAL PUSH
Pure pushing — hands on chest or shoulders, drive forward. No belt, no finesse. Just raw power. Crowd loves the thud.
Shitatenage
UNDERARM THROW
Inside belt grip throw. Lower risk for the thrower. Kirishima used this to seal many of his March wins.
Tsukiotoshi
SIDE PUSH DOWN
Push opponent diagonally down. Often looks like a stumble from outside but is deliberate. Watch the feet.

Mawashi = the belt wrestlers wear — gripping it is everything Kimarite = winning technique, announced after each bout Gyōji = the referee in ornate robes and hat

08 / 10 — The Best Moment

The Cushion Throw

When a lower-ranked wrestler defeats a Yokozuna or top Ozeki, the crowd throws their zabuton (floor cushions) into the air. It's called zabuton-nage and it's one of the most electrifying moments in all of sport.

🪑
What to Do
If it happens — duck slightly, watch for flying cushions, then look up and enjoy the chaos. Grab one if it lands near you as a souvenir. The crowd loves it.
🌊
When It Happens
Only when a Yokozuna or top Ozeki loses to someone ranked lower. With Ōnosato returning from injury — an upset is absolutely possible on Day 5.
🎯
Your Wrestler
If Yoshinofuji beats either Yokozuna today — this will happen. It will be extremely loud. It will be incredible. That's why he's your underdog.

Sound Like
a Real Fan

Basho
BAH-shoh
The tournament. You're at the Natsu (summer) Basho.
Rikishi
REE-kee-shee
A sumo wrestler. The proper term — not "sumo wrestler."
Dohyo
DOH-hyoh
The sacred clay ring. Raised 60cm off the floor. Women historically not permitted on it.
Yūshō
YOO-shoh
Tournament championship. The ultimate prize. 15-day record determines the winner.
Kenshō
KEN-shoh
Prize money envelopes — sponsors pay for these. Top bouts can have 50+ envelopes. Watch the gyōji wave them during the ritual.
Mono-ii
MOH-noh-ee
Judge consultation. If a call is disputed, 5 judges confer ringside. Sometimes results are reversed. Very dramatic.
🙏
Cheering: Japanese fans cheer loudly but briefly. "Yosha!" (yes!) and the wrestler's name are common. Don't jeer the loser — very poor form.
📵
No flash photography during bouts — it's distracting and disrespectful. Regular photos before and after are fine.
🍶
Eating and drinking during lower-division bouts is completely fine and normal. The arena bento + beer is a local tradition.
🏷️
Souvenirs: Buy from the stalls outside before entering — official merchandise, wrestler handprints (tegata), and printed programmes in English.
